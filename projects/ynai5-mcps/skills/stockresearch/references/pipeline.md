# STOCKRESEARCH — Pipeline Methodology

Full breakdown of each research phase. Apply these as direct analytical frameworks — think through each section and produce real analysis, not templates.

---

## PHASE 1 — Business Overview
*Run this when Solo doesn't fully know the company. Skip if they do.*

Goal: Understand what you're actually buying before looking at any numbers. A stock is ownership in a real business. Know the business first.

### 1A — What Does the Company Do?
Explain clearly:
- Core products or services (what do they sell?)
- Who are their customers? (consumers, enterprises, governments?)
- How do they make money? (revenue model — subscriptions, transactions, advertising, hardware, services?)
- Where do they operate? (domestic, multinational, emerging markets?)

Keep it plain. Avoid jargon. One paragraph, crisp.

### 1B — Competitive Advantage (What Makes It Special?)
This is the moat check. Rate it: **Strong / Moderate / Weak / None**

Ask: Why would a customer choose this company over a competitor? What would it take for someone to replace them?

Common moat types to check:
- **Brand** — Do people pay a premium because of the name? (Apple, Nike, Coca-Cola)
- **Switching costs** — Is it painful to leave? (enterprise software, banking, Adobe)
- **Network effects** — Does the product get more valuable as more people use it? (Visa, Meta, Airbnb)
- **Cost advantage** — Can they produce cheaper than anyone else? (Costco, Amazon logistics)
- **IP / Patents** — Do they own technology or formulas competitors can't copy? (pharma, semiconductors)
- **Regulatory moat** — Is there a license or regulation that limits competition? (utilities, defense contractors)

State the moat type and how durable it looks. Flag if there's no real moat — that changes the risk profile significantly.

### 1C — Industry Trend (Growing or Dying?)
Is this company in a growing industry, a mature/stable industry, or a declining one?

- **Growing** — Tailwind behind the business. Easier to grow revenue even without taking market share.
- **Mature** — Company must compete harder. Growth comes from winning share or cutting costs.
- **Declining** — Headwinds. Company needs to either pivot or is slowly losing relevance.

Name the industry trend and rate it: 🟢 Growing / 🟡 Mature / 🔴 Declining

Examples: AI infrastructure = 🟢. Traditional retail = 🟡. Print media = 🔴.

### 1D — Management (Who's in Charge?)
Briefly assess:
- Who is the CEO? How long have they been there?
- Are they a founder or a hired executive? (founders often more aligned with long-term value)
- Any major red flags — scandals, failed pivots, excessive dilution, poor capital allocation?
- Insider ownership: do executives own significant stock? High insider ownership = skin in the game.

Rate management: **Strong / Adequate / Concerning**

## PHASE 2 — Financial Statements
*The numbers that tell the truth about the business.*

Always cover all three statements. Each one tells a different part of the story.

### 2A — Income Statement
**What to look at:**

| Metric | What It Tells You | Green Signal | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue Growth (YoY) | Is the business actually growing? | >10% consistently | Declining or flat for 2+ years |
| Gross Margin | How profitable is the core product? | >40% for tech/SaaS, >20% for retail | Compressing margins over time |
| Operating Margin | How efficient is the whole business? | Positive and stable/growing | Negative or shrinking |
| Net Profit Margin | What actually hits the bottom line? | >10% (per the framework) | Negative or <5% for mature cos |
| EPS Trend | Is profit per share growing? | Rising YoY | Dilution hiding real performance |

Flag: Is revenue growth coming from price increases, volume, or new products? Price-led growth is weaker than volume-led.

### 2B — Balance Sheet
**What to look at:**

| Metric | What It Tells You | Green Signal | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cash & Equivalents | Can they survive a downturn? | Large cash buffer vs liabilities | Cash burn with no path to profitability |
| Total Debt | How leveraged are they? | Debt/Equity <1x for most sectors | High debt in a rising rate environment |
| Debt-to-Equity Ratio | Balance of debt vs equity funding | <1x (varies by industry) | >2x for non-financial companies |
| Current Ratio | Can they pay short-term obligations? | >1.5x | <1x = liquidity risk |
| Equity (Book Value) | What's the company worth on paper? | Growing over time | Negative equity = serious concern |

Flag: Some industries (banks, utilities) naturally carry more debt — context matters. Compare to sector peers.

### 2C — Cash Flow Statement
**This is the most important statement. Earnings can be manipulated. Cash flow is harder to fake.**

| Metric | What It Tells You | Green Signal | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operating Cash Flow | Is the core business generating real cash? | Positive and growing | Negative or declining |
| Capital Expenditure (CapEx) | How much do they reinvest to maintain/grow? | Reasonable % of revenue | CapEx eating all operating cash |
| Free Cash Flow (FCF) | Cash left after keeping the business running | Positive FCF = healthy | Negative FCF in mature companies |
| FCF Margin | FCF as % of revenue | >15% for quality businesses | <5% suggests thin cash generation |

**FCF Formula:** Free Cash Flow = Cash from Operations − CapEx

FCF is the lifeblood. A company that generates strong FCF can pay dividends, buy back stock, pay down debt, or reinvest in growth — without needing to borrow or dilute shareholders.

## PHASE 3 — Key Ratios
*Ratios put the numbers in context. Raw revenue means nothing without knowing what it cost to generate.*

### 3A — Return on Equity (ROE)
**Formula:** ROE = Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity

**What it means:** How efficiently is management using shareholder money to generate profit?

- ✅ ROE > 15% = strong (per the framework)
- 🟡 ROE 10–15% = acceptable
- 🔴 ROE < 10% = weak or capital-inefficient

**Watch out for:** Artificially high ROE caused by heavy debt (buybacks financed by borrowing inflate ROE). Check alongside debt levels.

### 3B — Net Profit Margin
**Formula:** Net Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue

**What it means:** For every $1 of revenue, how much is actual profit?

- ✅ Net Margin > 10% = healthy (per the framework)
- 🟡 Net Margin 5–10% = acceptable for most industries
- 🔴 Net Margin < 5% = thin, vulnerable to cost shocks

**Context matters:** Grocery stores operate on 1–3% margins by design. SaaS companies should be 20%+. Always compare to sector peers.

### 3C — Additional Efficiency Ratios (run these when relevant)

| Ratio | Formula | What It Measures | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Asset Turnover | Revenue ÷ Total Assets | How efficiently assets generate revenue | Higher = better, compare to peers |
| Inventory Turnover | COGS ÷ Avg Inventory | How fast they sell inventory | Higher = better (less capital tied up) |
| Interest Coverage | EBIT ÷ Interest Expense | Can they service their debt? | >3x = safe, <1.5x = danger zone |
| Revenue per Employee | Revenue ÷ Headcount | Productivity / scalability signal | Rising over time = efficiency improving |

## PHASE 4 — Key Risks
*Every stock has risks. The job is to name them clearly and decide if the reward justifies taking them.*

Always cover all three risk categories from the framework:

### 4A — Concentration Risk
Is the company dangerously dependent on one thing?

- **Customer concentration** — Does one customer account for >20% of revenue? Losing them would be catastrophic.
- **Product concentration** — Is one product responsible for >50% of revenue? One product failure = company failure.
- **Geographic concentration** — Is most revenue from one country? Political or economic risk is amplified.
- **Supplier concentration** — Is there one critical supplier with no backup? (Think chip shortage impact on automakers)

Rate: 🟢 Diversified / 🟡 Moderate Concentration / 🔴 High Concentration Risk

### 4B — Competition Risk
How safe is the market position?

- Who are the top 3 competitors?
- Is the company gaining or losing market share?
- Is there a new entrant threatening the space?
- Is pricing power holding or eroding?
- Could a larger company replicate what they do and crush them?

Rate: 🟢 Strong Position / 🟡 Competitive Market / 🔴 Under Pressure

### 4C — Disruption Risk
Could technology, regulation, or market shifts make this business obsolete?

- Is the core product/service at risk from AI, automation, or new technology?
- Is there pending regulation that could hurt the business model?
- Is consumer behavior shifting away from what they sell?
- Are there macro headwinds (rising rates, dollar moves, inflation) that specifically hurt this company?

Rate: 🟢 Low Disruption Risk / 🟡 Some Risk / 🔴 High Disruption Risk

**Overall Risk Summary:** After all three, give a combined risk rating: Low / Medium / High / Very High

## PHASE 5 — Valuation
*The price you pay determines your return. A great company at the wrong price is a bad investment.*

### 5A — P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
**Formula:** P/E = Market Price ÷ EPS (Earnings Per Share)

**What it means:** How much are investors paying for each $1 of earnings?

| P/E Range | What It Usually Signals |
|---|---|
| <15 | Potentially undervalued or low-growth expectations |
| 15–25 | Fairly valued for a stable business |
| 25–40 | Growth premium — market expects strong earnings growth |
| >40 | High expectations baked in — any miss gets punished hard |
| Negative | Company is not yet profitable |

**Always compare P/E to:** (1) the stock's own historical average, (2) sector/industry peers, (3) S&P 500 average (~20–22x typically).

### 5B — P/FCF Ratio (Price-to-Free Cash Flow)
**Formula:** P/FCF = Market Price ÷ FCF per Share

**Why this matters more than P/E:** Earnings can be manipulated with accounting. Free cash flow is harder to fake. Many professional investors prefer P/FCF for this reason.

| P/FCF Range | Signal |
|---|---|
| <15 | Potentially cheap — strong cash generation relative to price |
| 15–25 | Fair value range |
| 25–40 | Growth premium |
| >40 | Expensive — requires high growth to justify |

### 5C — Fair Value Estimate
Based on the above, give a rough fair value assessment:

- **Undervalued** — Current price is meaningfully below what the fundamentals suggest (>15% discount to fair value)
- **Fairly Valued** — Price is in line with fundamentals (within ~10%)
- **Overvalued** — Price is stretched beyond what the numbers justify (>15% premium to fair value)

Flag what live data is needed for precision: current stock price, latest EPS, trailing twelve months FCF.

### 5D — Margin of Safety Check
Would you still feel comfortable buying at this price if the stock dropped 20% more? If yes — the margin of safety is reasonable. If no — the entry is too aggressive.

---

## FINAL VERDICT

After all 5 phases, render a clear decision:

**🟢 BUY** — Strong fundamentals, reasonable valuation, manageable risks, clear growth story. Risk/reward favors entry.

**🟡 WATCH** — Interesting company but either: (a) valuation is stretched, (b) risks are elevated, or (c) need to wait for a better entry price or more data. Add to watchlist.

**🔴 AVOID** — Weak fundamentals, poor moat, high risk, or overvalued at current levels. Not worth the capital right now.

Always follow the verdict with 3 specific reasons. No vague statements — point to the exact metrics or facts that drove the call.
